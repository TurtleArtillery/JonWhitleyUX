Hiker Pro

I was inspired to create this design by my current running app. I won’t tell you which one I use because, well, it inspired me by crashing rather badly. I designed this app as a bit of a therapeutic exercise after the app I currently used crashed for probably the dozenth time. I use it nearly every day but it has brought me a lot of frustration. It crashes frequently, which is not a design problem, but it’s also crammed to the gills with superfluous social media functions that just get in the way. I mean, come on guys, who really wants to share a selfie of themselves looking all haggard and sweaty? Maybe it’s a great function for the top-tier athletes and models who advertise it, but the post-run look would not be a good look for me. I might switch to another app, but I already have over 2500 miles logged.

I ultimately decided to design an app geared toward adventure hiking and there were three key features I knew I really wanted to have with this app: distance traveled, GPS tagged route tracking, and a 3D topographic map. Beyond that I did some research to determine what other metrics and features would be good to include.

There really aren’t many well-designed hiking apps on the market right now, so it was tough to do research specifically on them; the next best thing turned out to be running apps. There are several quite well designed running apps that, of course, you could co-opt to use while walking – in fact, almost all of the major shoe brands have one. Common practices noted were: a start/pause/stop button, pace tracking, heart-rate, elapsed time, photo tagging, and distance traveled. Some of these I wanted, some not. Pace tracking could be a neat metric to record, but qualitative color grading is almost counter-productive in adventure-hiking, where it is as much or more about the journey as the exercise. Heart rate seemed significantly less important in a hiking app; you aren’t going to hit your “target heart-rate” by hiking unless you live in Colorado. But the photo tagging and start/pause/stop buttons gave me some good ideas to work with.

My final features list: start/pause/stop buttons, GPS and Elevation tracking, distance tracking, elapsed time, photo tagging, and old hike reviewing.

During the design phase the “pause” button evolved into a “camp” button to pause the tracking and save the current data to the disk so that a hiker can turn their phone off for the night to save battery without having to split their journey into multiple “hikes” within the app. I also added a button to create a “waypoint” on the map; this function would work similarly to taking a picture – it would simply set down a GPS point on the map and allow you to write a brief note about that point and time on your journey. For the more secondary features, account settings and so forth, I opted for the ever-popular top-right hamburger menu. For a color scheme I decided to use colors reminiscent of my own hiking days with the Scouts; classic color choices back then included a muted, hunter-green and a darker brick red. I also included an inlaid compass, because with a compass overlaid on a topographical map, it’s a lot harder to get lost.

I quite like the final product, made entirely in Sketch. I concluded this project with some nice, full-color renders and a few new handmade icons to add to my library.
